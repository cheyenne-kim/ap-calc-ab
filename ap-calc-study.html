Unit 1: Limits & Continuity

Exam weight: 10–12%
A limit describes what a function approaches as x nears a value — not necessarily what it equals there. Continuity at x=a requires: f(a) exists, the limit exists, and they are equal.
▶ Unit 1 TutorialLimits and Continuity ReviewMath & Coding Tutor · YouTube

One-Sided Limits

\(\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)\) and \(\lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)\)

Two-sided limit exists only if both sides agree

L'Hôpital's Rule

\(\lim\dfrac{f}{g}=\lim\dfrac{f'}{g'}\)

Only for \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty\) indeterminate forms

Special Trig Limits

\(\lim_{x\to 0}\dfrac{\sin x}{x}=1\)

\(\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{1-\cos x}{x}=0\)

End Behavior

\(\lim_{x\to\infty}\dfrac{c}{x^n}=0\)

Equal degrees → ratio of leading coefficients

IVT

\(f\) continuous on \([a,b]\)

\(f\) must take every value between \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\)

Continuity

\(f(a)=\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\)

Removable, jump, and infinite discontinuities

1
Easy
Evaluate: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3}\frac{x^2-9}{x-3}\)
Answer: C) 6
Factor: (x²−9) = (x−3)(x+3), so the expression = (x+3) for x≠3.
lim[x→3] (x+3) = 6
2
Medium
Evaluate: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin(3x)}{x}\)
Answer: C) 3
Rewrite: sin(3x)/x = 3·sin(3x)/(3x). As x→0, sin(3x)/(3x)→1.
Result: 3·1 = 3. (Or L'Hôpital: 3cos(3x)/1 = 3 at x=0)
3
Medium
For what value of \(k\) is \(f(x)\) continuous at \(x=2\)?
\[f(x)=\begin{cases}kx+1 & x\le2\\ x^2-1 & x>2\end{cases}\]
Answer: A) k = 1
Right-hand limit: lim[x→2⁺] x²−1 = 4−1 = 3
Left-hand value: f(2) = k(2)+1 = 2k+1
Set equal: 2k+1 = 3 → 2k = 2 → k = 1
4
Medium
Evaluate: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{3x^2+5x}{7x^2-2}\)
Answer: B) 3/7
Divide by x²: (3 + 5/x)/(7 − 2/x²). As x→∞, the extra terms vanish.
= 3/7. Shortcut: equal degrees → ratio of leading coefficients.
5
Hard
\(f(x)=\dfrac{x^2-4}{x-2}\) for \(x\neq2\), and \(f(2)=5\). Which statement is true?
Answer: C) Removable discontinuity
lim[x→2] (x²−4)/(x−2) = lim (x+2) = 4, but f(2) = 5.
The limit exists (= 4) but doesn't equal f(2), so there's a removable discontinuity. Redefining f(2) = 4 would make it continuous.
6
Easy
Evaluate: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\frac{x^2+3x-10}{x-2}\)
Answer: C) 7
Factor numerator: \(x^2+3x-10=(x-2)(x+5)\).
\(\lim_{x\to2}(x+5)=\)7
7
Easy
Evaluate: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to\infty}\frac{5x^3-2}{3x^3+x}\)
Answer: A) 5/3
Same degree → ratio of leading coefficients: \(5/3\).
(Divide numerator and denominator by \(x^3\) to confirm.)
8
Medium
For what value of \(k\) is \(f(x)\) continuous at \(x=1\)? \[f(x)=\begin{cases}2x+k & x<1\\ x^2+3 & x\ge1\end{cases}\]
Answer: B) k = 2
\(f(1)=1^2+3=4\). Left-hand limit: \(\lim_{x\to1^-}(2x+k)=2+k\).
Set equal: \(2+k=4\Rightarrow\)k = 2
9
Medium
The IVT guarantees a root of \(f(x)=x^3-2\) in which interval? (f is continuous everywhere.)
Answer: C) (1,2) because f(1) < 0 and f(2) > 0
\(f(1)=1-2=-1<0\) and \(f(2)=8-2=6>0\). Since \(f\) is continuous and changes sign, by the IVT a root exists in \((1,2)\).
10
Hard
Evaluate: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\sin(5x)}{\sin(3x)}\)
Answer: B) 5/3
Multiply and divide: \(\dfrac{\sin5x}{5x}\cdot\dfrac{3x}{\sin3x}\cdot\dfrac{5}{3}\).
Each \(\sin u/u\to1\) as \(x\to0\), so the limit is \(1\cdot1\cdot\dfrac{5}{3}=\)5/3

Unit 2: Differentiation — Basics

Exam weight: 10–12%
The derivative f′(x) = lim[h→0] (f(x+h)−f(x))/h measures the instantaneous rate of change and the slope of the tangent line.
▶ Unit 2 TutorialDerivatives and Basic Rules ReviewMath & Coding Tutor · YouTube

Power Rule

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^n]=nx^{n-1}\)

Product Rule

\((fg)'=f'g+fg'\)

Quotient Rule

\(\left(\dfrac{f}{g}\right)'=\dfrac{f'g-fg'}{g^2}\)

sin & cos

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sin x]=\cos x\)

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\cos x]=-\sin x\)

tan & sec

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\tan x]=\sec^2 x\)

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\sec x]=\sec x\tan x\)

eˣ & ln x

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x]=e^x\)

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln x]=\dfrac{1}{x}\)

1
Easy
Find \(f'(x)\) if \(f(x)=4x^3-7x^2+2x-5\)
Answer: A) 12x² − 14x + 2
Power rule term by term: 4·3x² = 12x², −7·2x = −14x, 2·1 = 2, −5 → 0.
f′(x) = 12x² − 14x + 2
2
Medium
Find \(dy/dx\) if \(y=x^2\sin x\)
Answer: B) 2x·sin(x) + x²·cos(x)
Product rule: f=x², f′=2x; g=sinx, g′=cosx.
dy/dx = (2x)(sinx) + (x²)(cosx)
3
Medium
Find the equation of the tangent line to \(f(x)=x^3-2x\) at \(x=1\).
Answer: A) y = x − 2
f(1) = 1−2 = −1 (point: (1,−1)). f′(x) = 3x²−2, so f′(1) = 1 (slope).
Tangent: y − (−1) = 1(x − 1) → y = x − 2
4
Hard
Differentiate: \(g(x)=\dfrac{x^2+1}{2x-3}\)
Answer: A) (2x²−6x−2)/(2x−3)²
Quotient rule: f=x²+1, f′=2x; g=2x−3, g′=2.
g′(x) = (2x(2x−3) − (x²+1)·2)/(2x−3)²
= (4x²−6x − 2x²−2)/(2x−3)² = (2x²−6x−2)/(2x−3)²
5
Easy
Differentiate: \(f(x)=3e^x+2\ln x\)
Answer: A) 3eˣ + 2/x
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x]=e^x\) and \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\ln x]=\dfrac{1}{x}\).
\(f'(x)=3e^x+\dfrac{2}{x}\)
6
Medium
Find \(f'(x)\) if \(f(x)=x^3 e^x\).
Answer: B) eˣ(x³ + 3x²)
Product rule: \((x^3)'e^x + x^3(e^x)' = 3x^2e^x + x^3e^x = e^x(3x^2+x^3)\)
7
Medium
If \(f(x)=x^4\), find \(f''(x)\).
Answer: B) 12x²
\(f'(x)=4x^3\), then \(f''(x)=12x^2\).
8
Medium
What is the slope of the tangent line to \(y=\ln x\) at \(x=e\)?
Answer: C) 1/e
\(y'=\dfrac{1}{x}\). At \(x=e\): slope \(=\dfrac{1}{e}\).
9
Hard
If \(f(x)=x\cos x\), find \(f'\!\left(\dfrac{\pi}{2}\right)\).
Answer: D) −π/2
Product rule: \(f'(x)=\cos x - x\sin x\).
\(f'(\pi/2)=\cos(\pi/2)-(\pi/2)\sin(\pi/2)=0-\pi/2=\)\(-\pi/2\)

Unit 3: Differentiation — Chain, Implicit, Inverse

Exam weight: 9–13%
The chain rule handles composite functions. Implicit differentiation lets you find dy/dx without solving for y. Inverse function derivatives flip the original derivative.
▶ Unit 3 TutorialChain Rule, Implicit, and Inverse DerivativesMath & Coding Tutor · YouTube

Chain Rule

\([f(g(x))]'=f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\)

Derivative of outer × derivative of inner

Implicit Diff

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[y^2]=2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}\)

Treat \(y\) as a function of \(x\); apply chain rule

Inverse Function

\((f^{-1})'(a)=\dfrac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(a))}\)

Slope of inverse = reciprocal of slope of \(f\)

arcsin & arctan

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\arcsin x]=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\)

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\arctan x]=\dfrac{1}{1+x^2}\)

aˣ & logax

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[a^x]=a^x\ln a\)

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\log_a x]=\dfrac{1}{x\ln a}\)

Higher-Order Deriv.

\(f''(x)=\dfrac{d}{dx}[f'(x)]\)

Second derivative: rate of change of the rate of change

1
Easy
Differentiate: \(h(x)=\sin(x^3)\)
Answer: B) 3x²·cos(x³)
Chain rule: outer = sin(u), inner u = x³.
h′(x) = cos(x³) · 3x² = 3x²cos(x³)
2
Medium
Differentiate: \(f(x)=e^{3x^2}\)
Answer: C) 6x·e^(3x²)
Chain: d/dx[eᵘ] = eᵘ·u′, where u = 3x², u′ = 6x.
f′(x) = e^(3x²)·6x = 6x·e^(3x²)
3
Medium
Find \(dy/dx\) using implicit differentiation: \(x^2+y^2=25\)
Answer: B) −x/y
Differentiate both sides: 2x + 2y(dy/dx) = 0
Solve: dy/dx = −2x/2y = −x/y
4
Medium
Find \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\arctan(2x)]\).
Answer: B) 2/(1+4x²)
d/dx[arctan(u)] = u′/(1+u²). Here u = 2x, u′ = 2.
= 2/(1+(2x)²) = 2/(1+4x²)
5
Hard
If \(f(3)=7\) and \(f'(3)=4\), find \((f^{-1})'(7)\).
Answer: C) 1/4
Inverse derivative formula: (f⁻¹)′(a) = 1/f′(f⁻¹(a)).
Since f(3) = 7, we know f⁻¹(7) = 3.
(f⁻¹)′(7) = 1/f′(3) = 1/4
6
Easy
Find \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\ln(3x^2+1)\right]\).
Answer: B) 6x/(3x²+1)
Chain rule with \(\ln(u)\): \(\dfrac{u'}{u}=\dfrac{6x}{3x^2+1}\)
7
Medium
Differentiate: \(f(x)=(\sin x)^4\)
Answer: C) 4sin³(x)cos(x)
Chain rule: outer \(u^4\), inner \(u=\sin x\).
\(f'=4(\sin x)^3\cdot\cos x\)
8
Medium
Find \(dy/dx\) using implicit differentiation for \(x^3+xy+y^2=7\) at the point \((2,1)\).
Answer: A) −13/4
Differentiate: \(3x^2+y+x\dfrac{dy}{dx}+2y\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0\).
At \((2,1)\): \(12+1+(2+2)\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0\Rightarrow\dfrac{dy}{dx}=\)\(-\dfrac{13}{4}\)
9
Medium
If \(g=f^{-1}\), \(f(2)=5\), and \(f'(2)=3\), find \(g'(5)\).
Answer: B) 1/3
\(g'(5)=\dfrac{1}{f'(g(5))}=\dfrac{1}{f'(2)}=\)\(\dfrac{1}{3}\)
(Since \(f(2)=5\Rightarrow g(5)=2\))
10
Hard
Use logarithmic differentiation to find \(y'\) if \(y=x^{\sin x}\).
Answer: A) x^(sinx)[cos(x)ln(x) + sin(x)/x]
Take ln: \(\ln y=\sin x\cdot\ln x\). Differentiate: \(\dfrac{y'}{y}=\cos x\ln x+\dfrac{\sin x}{x}\).
\(y'=x^{\sin x}\!\left(\cos x\ln x+\dfrac{\sin x}{x}\right)\)

Unit 4: Contextual Applications of Differentiation

Exam weight: 10–15%
Apply derivatives to real-world situations: motion (position/velocity/acceleration), related rates, local linearity (tangent line approximation), and L'Hôpital's Rule.
▶ Unit 4 TutorialContextual Applications of Differentiationdlpa · YouTube

Particle Motion

\(v(t)=s'(t),\quad a(t)=v'(t)\)

At rest: \(v=0\); speeding up: \(v\) and \(a\) same sign

Related Rates

Differentiate both sides w.r.t. \(t\)

Set up geometric equation, then differentiate implicitly

Linearization

\(L(x)=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)\)

Tangent line at \(x=a\) approximates \(f\) nearby

L'Hôpital's Rule

\(\lim\dfrac{f}{g}=\lim\dfrac{f'}{g'}\)

Applies when limit gives \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty\)

1
Easy
A particle's position is \(s(t)=t^3-6t^2+9t\). At what times is the particle at rest?
Answer: B) t = 1 and t = 3
v(t) = s′(t) = 3t²−12t+9 = 3(t−1)(t−3) = 0
At rest when t = 1 or t = 3
2
Medium
Use linearization to approximate \(\sqrt{9.1}\) using \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) at \(a=9\).
Answer: B) 3.017
f′(x) = 1/(2√x), so f′(9) = 1/6.
L(9.1) = f(9) + f′(9)(9.1−9) = 3 + (1/6)(0.1) = 3 + 0.0167 ≈ 3.017
3
Hard
A ladder 10 ft long leans against a wall. The bottom slides away at 2 ft/sec. How fast is the top sliding down when the bottom is 6 ft from the wall?
Answer: A) −3/2 ft/sec
x²+y²=100. Differentiate: 2x(dx/dt)+2y(dy/dt)=0.
When x=6: y=√(100−36)=8. Given dx/dt=2:
2(6)(2)+2(8)(dy/dt)=0 → dy/dt = −24/16 = −3/2 ft/sec
4
Hard
Evaluate using L'Hôpital's Rule: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{e^x-1-x}{x^2}\)
Answer: B) 1/2
At x=0: 0/0 form. Apply L'Hôpital: (eˣ−1)/(2x). Still 0/0 at x=0.
Apply again: eˣ/2. At x=0: e⁰/2 = 1/2
5
Easy
A particle moves along the x-axis with \(v(t)=3t^2-12\). For \(t\ge0\), when is the particle moving in the negative direction?
Answer: B) 0 < t < 2
\(v(t)=3(t^2-4)=3(t-2)(t+2)<0\) when \(-2For \(t\ge0\): negative direction on \((0,2)\).
6
Medium
A particle has position \(s(t)=t^3-6t^2+9t\). On which intervals is the particle speeding up?
Answer: D) (1,2) and (3,∞)
\(v(t)=3(t-1)(t-3)\): positive for \(t<1\) or \(t>3\), negative for \(1\(a(t)=6(t-2)\): positive for \(t>2\), negative for \(t<2\).
Speeding up when \(v\) and \(a\) have the same sign: \((1,2)\) and \((3,\infty)\).
7
Hard
Water drains from a conical tank (height 10 ft, radius 4 ft) at 2 ft³/min. How fast is the water level falling when \(h=5\) ft?
Answer: B) −1/(2π) ft/min
Since \(r/h=4/10\Rightarrow r=2h/5\). So \(V=\dfrac{4\pi}{75}h^3\).
\(\dfrac{dV}{dt}=\dfrac{4\pi}{25}h^2\dfrac{dh}{dt}\). At \(h=5\): \(-2=\dfrac{4\pi}{25}(25)\dfrac{dh}{dt}=4\pi\dfrac{dh}{dt}\Rightarrow\dfrac{dh}{dt}=\)\(-\dfrac{1}{2\pi}\)
8
Medium
Evaluate using L'Hôpital's Rule: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0}\frac{\tan x}{x}\)
Answer: B) 1
0/0 form. L'Hôpital: \(\lim_{x\to0}\dfrac{\sec^2 x}{1}=1\).
Alternatively: \(\dfrac{\tan x}{x}=\dfrac{\sin x}{x}\cdot\dfrac{1}{\cos x}\to1\cdot1=\)1
9
Medium
A particle has \(v(t)=2-t\) for \(0\le t\le4\). What is the total distance traveled?
Answer: C) 4
\(v=0\) at \(t=2\). Split: \(\displaystyle\int_0^2(2-t)\,dt+\int_2^4(t-2)\,dt=2+2=\)4
Note: displacement \(=\int_0^4(2-t)\,dt=0\), but distance \(\ne\) displacement when \(v\) changes sign.

Unit 5: Analytic Applications of Differentiation

Exam weight: 15–18% ★ High priority
Use derivatives to analyze function behavior: where it increases/decreases, where it curves, and where extrema occur. Know MVT and EVT cold — they appear every year.
▶ Unit 5 TutorialExtrema, Concavity, and Optimization ReviewMath & Coding Tutor · YouTube

MVT

\(f'(c)=\dfrac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\)

Requires \(f\) continuous on \([a,b]\), differentiable on \((a,b)\)

EVT

\(f\) continuous on \([a,b]\)

Guarantees an absolute max and min exist

1st Derivative Test

\(f':+\to-\Rightarrow\) local max

\(f':-\to+\Rightarrow\) local min; no sign change → neither

2nd Derivative Test

\(f'(c)=0,\;f''(c)>0\Rightarrow\) min

\(f''(c)<0\Rightarrow\) max; \(f''(c)=0\) inconclusive

Concavity

\(f''>0\Rightarrow\) concave up

Inflection point: \(f''\) changes sign

Absolute Extrema

Check critical points + endpoints

Compare \(f\)-values at all candidates on \([a,b]\)

1
Easy
Find the critical points of \(f(x)=x^3-3x^2-9x+5\).
Answer: D) x = −1 and x = 3
f′(x) = 3x²−6x−9 = 3(x²−2x−3) = 3(x−3)(x+1) = 0
Critical points: x = −1 and x = 3
2
Medium
For \(f(x)=x^2-3x\) on \([1,4]\), the MVT guarantees a \(c\in(1,4)\) where \(f'(c)=\)?
Answer: B) 2
MVT: (f(4)−f(1))/(4−1) = (4−(−2))/3 = 6/3 = 2
Verify: f′(x) = 2x−3 = 2 → x = 2.5 ∈ (1,4) ✓
3
Medium
On what interval(s) is \(f(x)=x^3-3x^2-9x+5\) increasing?
Answer: C) (−∞, −1) and (3, ∞)
f′(x) = 3(x+1)(x−3). Sign chart: f′ > 0 when both factors same sign.
Both negative: x < −1; both positive: x > 3. So f increases on (−∞,−1) ∪ (3,∞)
4
Hard
Find the absolute maximum of \(f(x)=-x^2+4x+1\) on \([0,5]\).
Answer: C) f = 6 at x = 2
f′(x) = −2x+4 = 0 → x = 2 (critical point).
Evaluate: f(0)=1, f(2)=−4+8+1=5... wait: f(2)=−4+8+1=5. f(5)=−25+20+1=−4.
Correction: f(2) = −(4)+8+1 = 5. Answer is B) f=5 at x=2.
5
Hard
A farmer has 200 ft of fencing to enclose a rectangular area against a barn (one side needs no fence). What dimensions maximize the area?
Answer: B) 50 × 100 ft, area = 5000 ft²
Let width = x (two sides), length = y (one side along barn).
Constraint: 2x + y = 200 → y = 200−2x.
Area A = x·y = x(200−2x) = 200x−2x².
A′ = 200−4x = 0 → x = 50, y = 100.
Max area = 50·100 = 5000 ft²
6
Easy
If \(f''(x)=6x-12\), on what interval is \(f\) concave up?
Answer: C) (2, ∞)
Concave up where \(f''>0\): \(6x-12>0\Rightarrow x>2\).
So \(f\) is concave up on \((2,\infty)\).
7
Medium
Find the x-coordinate of the inflection point of \(f(x)=2x^3-9x^2+12x-4\).
Answer: B) x = 3/2
\(f'=6x^2-18x+12\), \(f''=12x-18=0\Rightarrow x=\dfrac{3}{2}\).
Check sign change: \(f''(1)=-6<0\) and \(f''(2)=6>0\). Inflection at \(\)\(x=\tfrac{3}{2}\).
8
Medium
If \(f'(x)=(x-1)^2(x+2)\), how many local minima does \(f\) have?
Answer: A) 1 local minimum (at x = −2 only)
Sign of \(f'=(x-1)^2(x+2)\): negative for \(x<-2\), positive for \(x>-2\) (since \((x-1)^2\ge0\)).
\(f'\) changes \(-\to+\) at \(x=-2\) (local min). At \(x=1\): \(f'\) stays positive — no sign change, so no extremum.
9
Medium
Rolle's Theorem applies to \(f(x)=x^2-4x+3\) on \([1,3]\). What is the value of \(c\) guaranteed?
Answer: B) c = 2
\(f(1)=0=f(3)\). Rolle's conditions satisfied. \(f'(x)=2x-4=0\Rightarrow\)c = 2∈(1,3) ✓
10
Hard
Find the local minimum value of \(f(x)=\dfrac{x^3}{3}-x^2-3x+5\).
Answer: D) −4
\(f'=x^2-2x-3=(x-3)(x+1)=0\Rightarrow x=-1\) or \(x=3\).
\(f''=2x-2\): \(f''(3)=4>0\Rightarrow\) local min at \(x=3\).

Unit 6: Integration and Accumulation of Change

Exam weight: 17–20% ★ Highest priority
Integration accumulates change. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus is the most important result: it connects differentiation and integration. Master Riemann sums, FTC both parts, and u-substitution.
▶ Unit 6 TutorialRiemann Sums, FTC, Integrals, and U-SubMath & Coding Tutor · YouTube

Riemann Sum (Right)

\(\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i)\,\Delta x\)

Approximates area under a curve using rectangles

Power Rule (∫)

\(\displaystyle\int x^n\,dx=\dfrac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C\)

For \(n\neq -1\); don't forget \(+C\)

FTC Part 1

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\displaystyle\int_a^x\!f(t)\,dt\right]=f(x)\)

With chain rule: multiply by \(g'(x)\) if upper bound is \(g(x)\)

FTC Part 2

\(\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a)\)

\(F\) is any antiderivative of \(f\)

U-Substitution

\(u=g(x),\quad du=g'(x)\,dx\)

Change bounds too for definite integrals

Key Antiderivatives

\(\int e^x dx=e^x+C\)

\(\int\sin x\,dx=-\cos x+C\) · \(\int\frac{1}{x}dx=\ln|x|+C\)

1
Easy
Evaluate: \(\displaystyle\int(3x^2+4x-1)\,dx\)
Answer: B) x³ + 2x² − x + C
∫3x² = x³, ∫4x = 2x², ∫−1 = −x.
Result: x³ + 2x² − x + C
2
Easy
Evaluate: \(\displaystyle\int_0^2(x^2+1)\,dx\)
Answer: C) 14/3
F(x) = x³/3 + x. F(2)−F(0) = (8/3+2)−0 = 8/3+6/3 = 14/3

Graph of f′(x) Problems

AP Exam favorite — appears almost every year
On the AP exam you are often given a graph of f′(x) and asked questions about f(x) itself — its intervals of increase/decrease, local extrema, concavity, and inflection points. Remember: the graph you see IS the derivative, not the original function.
Key rules: f is increasing where f′ > 0 (above x-axis) · f is decreasing where f′ < 0 · local max of f where f′ changes + to − · local min where f′ changes − to + · f is concave up where f′ is increasing · inflection point of f where f′ changes from increasing to decreasing (or vice versa)
1
Easy
On what interval(s) is f(x) increasing?
Answer: B) (−2, 2) and (5, 6)
f(x) is increasing wherever f′(x) > 0 — i.e., wherever the graph of f′ is above the x-axis.
From the graph: f′ > 0 on (−2, 2) and on (5, 6).
f′ < 0 on (2, 5), so f is decreasing there.
2
Easy
At what x-value does f(x) have a local maximum?
Answer: C) x = 2
A local maximum of f occurs where f′ changes from positive to negative (1st Derivative Test).
At x = 2: f′ crosses the x-axis from above (f′ was positive, becomes negative). → local max of f
At x = 5: f′ crosses from below (negative to positive) → that's a local min of f.
At x = 0 and x = 3: f′ ≠ 0 there — those are where f′ peaks/troughs, not zeros.
3
Medium
On what interval is f(x) concave down?
Answer: B) (0, 3)
f is concave down where f′′ < 0 — i.e., where the graph of f′ is decreasing.
From the graph: f′ reaches its peak at x = 0 and decreases until its trough at x = 3.
So f′ is decreasing on (0, 3) → f is concave down on (0, 3).
On (−2, 0) and (3, 6): f′ is increasing → f is concave up there.
4
Medium
At which x-value(s) does f(x) have an inflection point?
Answer: D) x = 0 and x = 3
Inflection points of f occur where f′′ changes sign — i.e., where the graph of f′ changes from increasing to decreasing (or vice versa).
• At x = 0: f′ switches from increasing → decreasing (local max of f′) → f changes from concave up to concave down → inflection point
• At x = 3: f′ switches from decreasing → increasing (local min of f′) → f changes from concave down to concave up → inflection point
x = 2 and x = 5 are zeros of f′ (extrema of f), not inflection points.
5
Hard
Which statement about f(x) at x = 4 is correct?
Answer: C) f is concave up at x = 4
Check each option using the graph of f′:
A) Local min requires f′ = 0 and sign change — f′(4) ≠ 0, so no extremum. ✗
B) f increasing requires f′ > 0 — but f′(4) < 0 (below x-axis between x=2 and x=5). ✗
C) f concave up requires f′ to be increasing at x=4. After the trough at x=3, f′ is increasing on (3,6). So f′′(4) > 0 → f is concave up. ✓
D) Inflection requires f′ to change from increasing↔decreasing — f′ is only increasing at x=4, no sign change in f′′. ✗
6
Hard
Suppose f(1) = 3. Which of the following is a possible value for f(3)?
Answer: D) 1
From x=1 to x=3: f′(x) is positive from x=1 to x=2 (so f increases), then negative from x=2 to x=3 (so f decreases).
Starting at f(1)=3: f rises to a local max at x=2, then falls back down by x=3.
The area under f′ from 1 to 2 is positive, and from 2 to 3 is negative and larger in magnitude (f′ dips to −2 at x=3 vs max of ~2).
So f(3) < f(1) = 3. Options A and B (≥5) are too large; C (=3) would require the areas to cancel, but the trough is deeper. D) 1 is a plausible value less than 3.
On the actual AP exam you would compute using the definite integral of f′ over [1,3].
7
Easy
At what x-value does f(x) have a local minimum?
Answer: C) x = 5
A local minimum of f occurs where f′ changes from negative to positive (1st Derivative Test).
At x = 5: f′ crosses the x-axis from below → f′ goes from negative to positive → local min of f.
At x = 2: f′ goes from positive to negative → local max of f. At x = 0 and x = 3: f′ ≠ 0, so those aren't extrema of f.
8
Easy
On what interval is f(x) decreasing?
Answer: B) (2, 5)
f is decreasing wherever f′(x) < 0 — i.e., where the curve is below the x-axis.
From the graph, f′ < 0 on (2, 5). On all other parts of [−2, 6] the curve is above the x-axis, so f is increasing there.
9
Medium
On what interval is f(x) both decreasing and concave down?
Answer: B) (2, 3)
f is decreasing where f′ < 0 → (2, 5).
f is concave down where f′ is decreasing → (0, 3).
The intersection of those two intervals is (2, 3) — the only region where both conditions hold simultaneously.
10
Medium
On what interval is f(x) both decreasing and concave up?
Answer: C) (3, 5)
f is decreasing where f′ < 0 → (2, 5).
f is concave up where f′ is increasing → (−2, 0) and (3, 6).
Intersection of (2,5) and (3,6): (3, 5) — the region where f is falling but curving upward (like the right side of a valley).
11
Medium
Which statement best describes f(x) at \(x=0\)?
Answer: B) f is increasing and has an inflection point at x = 0
At x = 0: f′(0) = 3 > 0 → f is increasing. (No extremum — f′ ≠ 0.)
Also at x = 0, f′ reaches its local maximum, meaning f′ switches from increasing to decreasing → f changes from concave up to concave down → inflection point of f.
This is the classic trap: x = 0 is an extremum of f′, but only an inflection point of f.
12
Medium
Which x-values on [−2, 6] are relative extrema of f(x)?
Answer: D) x = 2 and x = 5 only
Relative extrema of f require f′ = 0 and a sign change in f′.
• x = 2: f′ = 0, sign changes + → − → local max of f ✓
• x = 5: f′ = 0, sign changes − → + → local min of f ✓
• x = 0 and x = 3: These are peaks/troughs of f′ (f′ ≠ 0 there), so they are inflection points of f, not extrema.

By the Net Change Theorem: \(f(2)=f(-2)+\displaystyle\int_{-2}^{2}f'(x)\,dx\).
From the graph, f′(x) > 0 for all x in (−2, 2), so \(\int_{-2}^{2}f'\,dx>0\).
Therefore \(f(2)=2+(\text{positive number})>\)2.

Hard

• f increases on (−2, 2) → f(2) > f(−2), so x = −2 is not the max.
• f decreases on (2, 5) → f(5) < f(2).
• f increases on (5, 6), but f′ stays small (<1) on that interval, so f(6) = f(5) + (small positive) << f(2).
The Net Change from 2 to 6 is \(\int_2^6 f'\,dx\) = large negative area + small positive ≈ net negative.
So f(6) < f(2), confirming the absolute maximum is at x = 2.

Name the theorem or test you're using. Answers without justification receive no credit for the reasoning points even if the value is correct.

Implicit Differentiation

Unit 3 · 9–13% — heavily tested on FRQ
When \(y\) cannot easily be isolated, differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\). Every time you differentiate a term involving \(y\), multiply by \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) (chain rule). Then solve for \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\).

Core Technique

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[y^n]=ny^{n-1}\dfrac{dy}{dx}\)

Chain rule: y is a function of x, so multiply by dy/dx

Product with y

\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[xy]=y+x\dfrac{dy}{dx}\)

Product rule applies when both x and y appear

Solve for dy/dx

Collect \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) terms, factor out, divide

Group all dy/dx terms on one side, everything else on the other

Tangent Line

Evaluate \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) at \((x_0,y_0)\)

Plug in the point after finding dy/dx in general form

Horizontal Tangent

Set \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}=0\), solve

Then substitute back into original equation to find the points

Second Derivative

Differentiate \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\) implicitly again

Substitute dy/dx expression when it appears in d²y/dx²

Optimization

Unit 5 · Analytic Applications — classic calculator and no-calculator setup skill
Optimization problems ask you to maximize or minimize a quantity under a constraint. The workflow is almost always: define variables, write the target equation, use the constraint to get one variable, differentiate, solve, and justify the extremum.

Step 1

Draw a diagram and label variables.

State what each variable represents, including units when given.

Step 2

Write the objective function.

This is the quantity you want to maximize or minimize: area, volume, cost, distance, time, etc.

Step 3

Use the constraint to eliminate a variable.

Perimeter, volume, or geometry equations usually let you rewrite the objective in one variable.

Step 4

Differentiate and solve \(f'(x)=0\).

Also check endpoints if the domain is closed or restricted.

Step 5

Justify max or min.

Use sign changes, the second derivative test, or endpoint comparison.

Common Trap

Answer the question asked.

If it asks for dimensions, give dimensions. If it asks for max area, give area.

A rectangle has perimeter \(40\) units. What dimensions maximize its area?
Answer: B) \(10\times10\)
Let sides be \(x\) and \(y\). Constraint: \(2x+2y=40\Rightarrow y=20-x\).
Area: \(A=x(20-x)=20x-x^2\). \(A'=20-2x=0\Rightarrow x=10\), so \(y=10\). Since \(A''=-2<0\), this is a maximum.

Area: \(A=x(120-2x)=120x-2x^2\). \(A'=120-4x=0\Rightarrow x=30\), so \(y=60\). \(A''=-4<0\), so this maximizes area.

FRQ Practice

Free-response questions make up 50% of your AP score (6 questions in 90 minutes). Unlike MC, you must show full work — a correct answer without work earns zero. The rubric rewards each step, so partial credit is always possible. Practice writing complete, organized responses.
FRQ FormatSection II: 6 questions · 90 minutes total
• Part A (30 min, calculator): 2 problems — typically motion, area/volume, or applied integral
• Part B (60 min, no calculator): 4 problems — typically limits/derivatives, FTC, diff eq, curve analysis
Each problem is worth 9 points; each part (a)(b)(c)(d) is worth 2–4 points.

FRQ Scoring Principles

Show All Work

No work = no credit

Even if the answer is obvious, write the setup. Examiners award points for each step, not just the answer.

Import Error

Carry forward wrong values

If you get part (a) wrong but use it correctly in (b), you can still earn full credit on (b). Don't restart — keep going.

Units

Always include units

A rate without units (like "ft/sec") loses the unit point. Check every answer that has a real-world context.

Justify Conclusions

Say WHY

"f′(x) changes from + to − at x=2, so f has a local maximum there." State the reasoning, not just the conclusion.

Declare Variables

Related rates especially

Start with "Let x = ..." and "Let y = ..." before writing equations. Examiners look for this.

Round Correctly

3 decimal places

On calculator problems, give at least 3 decimal places unless told otherwise. Don't round intermediate steps.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Quick reminders for the errors that cost the most points on AP Calculus AB. Read this section before timed practice or FRQs.

Derivatives

Chain Rule

Differentiate the inside

For sin(x²), e^(3x), ln(2x), and (x²+1)^5, multiply by the derivative of the inner function.

Product Rule

(fg)' = f'g + fg'

Do not differentiate each factor and multiply the answers. Keep both product-rule terms.

Constants

d/dx[5] = 0

Constants disappear. e² is a constant; e^x is not.

Trig Signs

d/dx[cos x] = -sin x

The negative on cosine is easy to drop. Also remember d/dx[tan x] = sec²x.

Implicit Diff

d/dx[y²] = 2y dy/dx

Every term with y needs a dy/dx factor unless y is treated as a constant by the problem.

Quotient Rule

(f'g - fg') / g²

The subtraction order matters. A sign error can flip the whole numerator.

Integrals

Missing + C

Indefinite integral

Add + C until an initial condition or definite bound removes it.

u-Sub Bounds

Change the limits

For definite integrals, convert x-bounds to u-bounds or substitute back before evaluating.

dx Replacement

Replace the whole differential

If u = x², then du = 2x dx. Do not leave unmatched x terms behind.

Power Rule Exception

Integral of 1/x

Integral of x^-1 is ln|x| + C, not x^0 / 0.

FTC Chain Rule

Upper bound x²

d/dx of integral from 0 to x² of f(t) dt is f(x²) times 2x.

Flipped Limits

Reverse means negative

Integral from b to a equals the negative of the integral from a to b.

Theorems and FRQs

MVT / EVT

Check hypotheses

State continuity on the closed interval and differentiability on the open interval when needed.

Critical Points

f' = 0 is not enough

Use a sign change or another test before calling something a max or min.

Concavity

Use f''

Increasing and concave up are different ideas. Concavity comes from the second derivative.

Velocity, acceleration, area, volume, and rates all need correct units in context problems.

Distance vs. Displacement

Use absolute value for distance

Integral of velocity is displacement. Integral of speed is total distance.

Speeding Up

Same sign

A particle speeds up when velocity and acceleration have the same sign.
